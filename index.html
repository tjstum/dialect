How your map is calculated

This map runs on the Harvard Dialect Survey, a study of American English by Bert Vaux and Scott Golder that collected tens of thousands of responses to questions about regional vocabulary and pronunciation. For each of the questions used here, the survey recorded the percentage of respondents in every U.S. state who chose each answer.

1. Scoring each state. When you pick an answer, we look up how common that answer is in each state. Your similarity to a state is simply the average, across all the questions you’ve answered, of the probability that a randomly chosen resident of that state would answer the same way you did:

similarity(state) = average over your answers of  P( resident of state gives your answer )

2. Drawing the smooth map. Each state is placed at its geographic center and given its similarity score. To turn 51 scattered points into a continuous picture, we use Gaussian kernel smoothing: every point on the map is a weighted blend of the nearby state scores, where closer states count for more. The blended surface is then split into color bands — deep blue where people talk least like you, pale yellow in the middle, and red where they talk most like you.

Download the source data

The complete dataset behind this map is free to download. The CSV contains one row for every combination of state, question, and answer, with the percentage of that state’s respondents who chose it — 51 jurisdictions × 148 questions × their answer options (including 122 survey questions and 26 modeled questions).

Columns: state_code, state, question_id, question, answer_id, answer, percent
