      How your map is calculated

      This map runs on the Harvard Dialect Survey, a study of American English by Bert Vaux and Scott Golder that collected tens of thousands of responses to questions about regional vocabulary and pronunciation. For each of the 24 questions used here, the survey recorded the percentage of respondents in every U.S. state — plus Washington, D.C. — who chose each answer. Those state-by-state percentages are the raw material for everything below.

      1. Scoring each state. When you pick an answer, we look up how common that answer is in each state. Your similarity to a state is simply the average, across all the questions you’ve answered, of the probability that a randomly chosen resident of that state would answer the same way you did:

      similarity(state) = average over your answers of  P( resident of state gives your answer )

      A score of 60% means that, question by question, a typical person in that state matches your answers about 60% of the time. The more questions you answer, the sharper the estimate.

      2. Drawing the smooth map. Each state is placed at its geographic center and given its similarity score. To turn 51 scattered points into a continuous picture, we use Gaussian kernel smoothing: every point on the map is a weighted blend of the nearby state scores, where closer states count for more. The blended surface is then split into color bands — deep blue where people talk least like you, pale yellow in the middle, and red where they talk most like you — with the scale stretched to your own range so contrasts stay visible.

      3. Cities and “what gave you away.” Each city is scored by reading the smoothed surface at its exact location, which is why the most- and least-similar lists shift as you answer more questions. The “what gave you away” panel finds your single most similar state, then highlights the answers where your choice is far more common in that state than it is nationwide — the responses doing the most to pin you down.

      Limitations to keep in mind. Scores are anchored at state centers, so variation within a state (a big city versus its rural areas) is smoothed away. Alaska and Hawaii are colored from their own results and kept out of the mainland blend, since they have no nearby neighbors and relatively few survey responses. Rare or one-off answers are grouped into an “other” bucket. And the underlying survey is a large but self-selected sample, not a census — treat the map as a well-supported estimate, not a measurement.

      Download the source data

      The complete dataset behind this map is free to download. The CSV contains one row for every combination of state, question, and answer, with the percentage of that state’s respondents who chose it — 51 jurisdictions × 24 questions × their answer options.

      Columns: state_code, state, question_id, question, answer_id, answer, percent
